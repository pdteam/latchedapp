Poor Latch Technique


Characteristics of a Good Latch


The latch is a key component of the breastfeeding relationship. A latch is often a common denominator for many breastfeeding problems. A poor latch must be corrected as soon as possible.
If the mother has no pain, the baby is thriving and the milk is being transferred appropriately, there is no point in fixing a latch that looks bad. This may decrease the mother’s confidence in her breastfeeding skills and increase the risk of breastfeeding failure.
Only address a poor latch if it results in problems.

Tongue Tie

Babies with ankyloglossia (tongue ties) can present early if the tongue is severe, but can also present later (around 6 to 8 weeks of age). Mothers with abundant milk supply and rapid let-down can compensate for a poor latch in the first few weeks. As the prolactin levels decline after the first 10 postpartum days, mothers’ milk supply start to reflect the supply and demand balance more accurately and will decrease if the latch is poor / there is poor milk removal from the breast, which may be caused by a tongue tie.

If a baby has a tongue tie, he or she may not be able to extend the tongue past the gum line. If the baby cannot touch the roof of the mouth with his / her tongue, suspect a tongue tie.

Symptoms of Tongue Tie

  • 1) Slow weight gain
  • 2) Uncomfortable and gassy baby
  • 3) Gulping air while feeding
  • 4) Trauma to maternal nipples

Examples of Tongue Tie

Lingual frenulum appears short, the tongue does not raise much centrally when crying, it extends just over the gums but not the lips. The middle of the tongue does not raise as much as expected when swallowing.
The Hazelbaker Assessment for Lingual Frenulum Function evaluates the appearance of the tongue when lifted, the elasticity, length, and attachment locations of the frenulum. It also assesses the function of the tongue in regards to lateralization, lift, extension, cupping, peristalsis, and snapback.

Treatment of Tongue Tie

Tongue ties (ankyloglossia) should be clipped if they interfere with breastfeeding. A frenotomy is recommended and can be done by a dentist or a physician with experience in the procedure
Referral to a lactation consultant is highly recommended to help with the breastfeeding relationship before and after the frenotomy
Provide the mother with a list of breastfeeding clinics and other community resources.

Poor Weight Gain

Poor Milk Transfer


It is important to realize that a baby suckling at the breast is not necessarily transferring milk effectively and / or swallowing.
After lactogenesis II (when the milk is “in”), a soft swallowing sound is heard, indicating that the baby is transferring milk. The sound it makes is that of a soft “caw”.



Treatment of Poor Milk Transfer

In the case of poor milk transfer, suspect poor latch and / or insufficient milk supply
The use of breast compression while baby is breastfeeding will also promote milk transfer to baby.
Provide the mother with a list of breastfeeding clinics and other community resources

Scheduled Feedings


Babies should not be given a schedule. For instance, telling a mother to feed her infant every 3 hours for 15 minutes on each breast can lead to problems.
In a baby who is growing well, scheduled feedings are never indicated. It is important to follow the baby’s cues. A minimum number of 8 feedings per 24 hours is essential in the first two weeks, but feeding can be liberalized afterwards.



Treatment of Scheduled Feedings


Mothers should feed their baby on demand, and empty the first breast that the baby is taking, before switching onto the second breast
Breast compression may help with emptying the breast.
Provide the mother with a list of breastfeeding clinics and other community resources

Insufficient Milk Supply


Risk factors for delayed lactogenesis / low milk supply include polycystic ovarian syndrome, gestational diabetes, previous breast surgery.
Signs of insufficient milk supply include poor weight gain, poor milk transfer, low urine / stool output


Treating Low Milk Supply


If milk supply is deemed to be insufficient, the following steps can be followed :

1) Ensure the baby is offered the breast at least every 3 hours
2) Keep baby undressed and skin to skin for feedings
3) The mother should offer both breasts at each feeding, switching sides frequently as soon as the baby appears sleepy. The use of breast compression while baby is breastfeeding will promote milk transfer to the baby
4) If baby needs a supplement, ideally offer expressed breast milk, if available
5) Recommend doing hand expression then pumping for 15 minutes after every feed to increase stimulation to breast, and increase milk supply
6) Provide the mother with a list of breastfeeding clinics and other community resources

Maternal Confidence

Lack of Knowledge


Mothers may worry about breastfeeding even when breastfeeding is going well. A lack of knowledge with regards to normal feeding pattern may result in inappropriate concerns.
Frequent feedings are normal, especially in the evening
If the baby is growing, he / she will be going to the breast more often to increase milk supply to satisfy his / her increasing needs. This is normal and access to the breast should not be limited, as this is the only way the baby can increase milk supply
If feedings take less than 45 minutes in duration, and the baby is growing well, and all other aspects of breastfeeding are going well, it may be appropriate to reassure her with regards to normal feeding patterns.
Encourage the mother’s partner / support person to get involved by changing the baby, or brining the baby to the mother
Remember to assess mothers for post-partum depression, especially if she seems more tired than would be expected.

Mother does not Feel her Let-down


This is often not a problem. Up to 85% of women do not feel their let-down reflex. Asking mothers whether they feel their let-down reflex may actually be detrimental to their confidence as it suggests that there is a problem if they don’t. Most women do not feel the let-down reflex.

Mother's Breast Don't Feel Full


This may not be problem. Many women do not feel that their breasts are full at the beginning of a feed, and they may not feel that their breasts are empty at the end of a feed

Asking mothers whether they feel that their breasts are full or empty may actually be detrimental to their confidence as it suggests that there is a problem if they don’t.

The Baby is Fussy


There are many reasons for a baby to be fussy. Ensure the baby is gaining weight well, the milk transfer is adequate, and that the mother has no nipple pain while breastfeeding.

Other causes related to breastfeeding:

1) Scheduled feedings
2) Ankyloglossia: Consider other causes unrelated to breastfeeding
